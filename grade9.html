Criteria Guide

What A, B, C, and D mean in Grade 9 — with examples from your actual units.

A
Knowing & Understanding Select and apply mathematical knowledge

In Grade 9 this is harder than Grade 8. Unfamiliar problems appear — questions that use skills you know but combine them in ways you haven't seen before. Level 7–8 requires you to navigate those without freezing.

Key units: Shape of U (quadratic methods), Get Triggy (choosing sin/cos/tan vs sine/cosine rule), We Like to Argue (selecting the right congruence or similarity criterion).

Justify your method. "I used the quadratic formula because the expression did not factorise easily" is Level 7 thinking. Choosing a method with no explanation is not.

Level 5–6
Solves familiar multi-step problems correctly. Selects an appropriate method. Shows working clearly but may not explain why the method was chosen.
Level 7–8
Solves both familiar and unfamiliar problems. Selects the most efficient method, justifies the choice, and verifies the reasonableness of the answer.
B
Investigating Patterns Observe, generalise, and justify

In Grade 9 Crit B appears in Shape of U (quadratic pattern generalisation) and We Like to Argue / Drop a Pin (geometric pattern investigation). The expectation is higher than Grade 8.

At Level 7–8, your justification should use algebraic or geometric reasoning, not just "I tested five cases and it worked." Testing cases is verification. Explaining why it works for all cases is justification.

A common mistake: writing a general rule, verifying it, and stopping. The jump from Level 6 to Level 8 is almost always in the quality of the final justification.

Level 5–6
States a correct general rule. Verifies with at least one case not used to find the pattern. Attempts a justification but it may be informal or incomplete.
Level 7–8
States a correct general rule. Verifies it thoroughly. Provides a clear mathematical justification for why the rule holds — not just checks that it does.
C
Communicating Use mathematical language precisely

Grade 9 raises the notation bar. You're now working with trigonometric functions, set notation, discriminants, and geometric proofs — each with their own precise language.

In trigonometry: label diagrams fully, write angles with degree symbols, round to the stated accuracy. In probability: use set notation correctly (∩, ∪, P(A|B)). In geometry: name triangles consistently (△ABC), state theorems before applying them.

A complete proof that uses imprecise notation will lose marks. A correct answer without a clear diagram or unit will lose marks. Precision matters more each year.

Level 5–6
Uses mostly correct notation. Diagrams present and mostly labelled. Conclusions reference the context. Working is logical with minor gaps.
Level 7–8
Precise notation throughout. All diagrams fully labelled with units. Conclusions are mathematically precise. Working is complete and would be clear to any reader.
D
Applying in Real Life Model real situations — and reflect critically

Grade 9 Crit D tasks — Get Triggy and What Are the Chances? — require you to build a mathematical model of a real situation, not just apply a formula to a given context.

The reflection stage is where Grade 9 differs from Grade 8. You're now expected to evaluate your model's accuracy with mathematical specificity. "My calculation may be slightly off because I assumed the ground was flat" is a start. "My calculation assumes the ground is horizontal, but a 5° slope would reduce the calculated height by approximately cos(5°) × h" is Level 8.

Think: identify the situation → state your assumptions → build the model → interpret results → evaluate accuracy and limitations.

Level 5–6
Applies correct maths to the real context. Interprets results in terms of the situation. Identifies at least one assumption or limitation.
Level 7–8
Builds a complete model with explicitly stated assumptions. Interprets all results precisely in context. Critically evaluates the model's accuracy and limitations with mathematical justification.

QuadraticsExtended+

The Discriminant

Δ = b² − 4ac tells you the nature of roots before you solve. Positive → two real roots. Zero → one repeated root. Negative → no real roots.

Worked example
x² + 3x + 5 = 0 → Δ = 9 − 20 = −11 < 0
No real roots — the parabola doesn't cross the x-axis.
QuadraticsExtended+

Quadratic Inequalities

Solve by finding the roots first, then using the graph's shape (positive a = U-shape) to identify where the parabola is above or below zero.

Worked example
x² − x − 6 > 0 → (x−3)(x+2) > 0
Roots: x = 3, x = −2 → x < −2 or x > 3
QuadraticsAll streams

Completing the Square

Rewrite ax² + bx + c in vertex form a(x + h)² + k to read off the vertex directly and understand horizontal/vertical transformations.

Worked example
x² + 4x + 1 = (x+2)² − 3
→ Vertex at (−2, −3), translated left 2 and down 3.
QuadraticsFurther

Form an Equation from Roots

If you know the roots α and β, the quadratic is x² − (α+β)x + αβ = 0. Useful for checking solutions and constructing equations from geometric or applied contexts.

Worked example
Roots: 2 and −5 → x² − (2+(−5))x + (2×−5) = 0
→ x² + 3x − 10 = 0
TrigonometryAll streams

Right-Angled Trigonometry

Use sin, cos, and tan to find missing sides and angles. Always identify opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse relative to the angle you're working with.

Worked example
Angle = 35°, adjacent = 12: tan(35°) = opp/12
→ opp = 12 × tan(35°) ≈ 8.40
TrigonometryExtended+

Sine & Cosine Rules

For non-right triangles. Sine rule: a/sin A = b/sin B. Cosine rule: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A. Use sine rule when you have angle-side pairs; cosine rule when you have three sides or two sides and the included angle.

Which rule?
Two sides + included angle → cosine rule
Two angles + one side → sine rule
TrigonometryExtended+

Radians & the Unit Circle

Radians are an alternative measure of angle. π radians = 180°. The unit circle shows sin and cos as coordinates — sin θ is the y-coordinate, cos θ is the x-coordinate of the point at angle θ.

Key conversions
30° = π/6 · 45° = π/4 · 60° = π/3 · 90° = π/2 · 180° = π
TrigonometryFurther

Trigonometric Identities

Fundamental identities derived from the unit circle: sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and tan θ = sin θ / cos θ. Use these to simplify expressions and prove results.

Worked example
Show tan θ · cos θ = sin θ:
(sin θ/cos θ) · cos θ = sin θ ✓
ProbabilityAll streams

Conditional Probability

P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B) — the probability of A given B has already occurred. Read from tree diagrams, two-way tables, or Venn diagrams.

Worked example
P(rain) = 0.4, P(late|rain) = 0.7
P(late and rain) = 0.4 × 0.7 = 0.28
ProbabilityAll streams

Independent vs Dependent Events

Events are independent if P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B). Dependent events influence each other — sampling without replacement creates dependent events.

Quick test
Is P(A|B) = P(A)? If yes → independent
Does outcome of first affect second? → dependent
ProbabilityExtended+

Counting Principle & Combinations

Counting principle: multiply the number of choices at each stage. Combinations (nCr) count selections where order doesn't matter. Permutations (nPr) count arrangements where order matters.

Worked example
Choose 3 from 8 (order doesn't matter):
⁸C₃ = 8!/(3!×5!) = 56 ways
ProbabilityFurther

Binomial Distribution

Models repeated independent trials with two outcomes (success/failure). P(X=k) = nCk × p^k × (1−p)^(n−k). Expected value E(X) = np.

Worked example
Fair coin, 5 flips, P(exactly 3 heads):
⁵C₃ × (0.5)³ × (0.5)² = 10 × 0.125 × 0.25 = 0.3125
GeometryExtended+

Triangle Congruence & Similarity

Congruent triangles: same shape and size (SSS, SAS, ASA, RHS). Similar triangles: same shape, different size — corresponding sides in proportion. Always state which criterion you're using.

Key distinction
Congruent: all sides and angles equal
Similar: all angles equal, sides proportional
GeometryExtended+

Circle Theorems

Key theorems: angle at centre is twice angle at circumference; angles in the same segment are equal; angle in semicircle is 90°; tangent is perpendicular to radius at the point of contact.

Tip
Always state the theorem by name before applying it. This is Crit C marks as well as Crit A.
GeometryStandard

Coordinate Geometry (Drop a Pin)

Use distance formula √((x₂−x₁)²+(y₂−y₁)²), midpoint formula, and slope to solve geometry problems on coordinate planes. Apply to find perimeters and areas of polygons.

Worked example
Distance (2,1) to (5,5): √(9+16) = √25 = 5
Midpoint: ((2+5)/2, (1+5)/2) = (3.5, 3)
