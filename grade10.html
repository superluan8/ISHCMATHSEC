Criteria Guide

Grade 10 is your final MYP assessment year. The bar is higher — and the habits you build now are the ones you carry into DP.

A
Knowing & UnderstandingSelect, apply, and justify

Grade 10 Crit A tests whether you can select the right tool for unfamiliar problems. With four years of techniques now available — from linear equations to logarithms to vectors — the challenge is knowing which approach fits a new situation, not just executing a familiar one.

Key expectation at Level 7–8: you explain your method choice and check your answer makes sense in context. In Finance Matters, that means checking compound interest answers against reasonable estimates. In Triangles and Circles, it means checking whether an angle answer is geometrically plausible.

Level 5–6
Solves familiar and some unfamiliar problems. Shows method clearly. Selects appropriate techniques with minor inefficiencies.
Level 7–8
Solves unfamiliar multi-step problems. Selects the most efficient method, justifies it, and verifies the answer is reasonable in context.
B
Investigating PatternsGeneralise, verify, and justify formally

By Grade 10, Crit B justifications are expected to be algebraic or formally reasoned, not informal. "I checked five cases" is not Level 8 justification. A proof — even a simple one — or a clear algebraic argument for why the rule holds in general, is.

Finance Matters (sequence generalisation) and Triangles and Circles (geometric pattern investigation) are the key Crit B units. In Further, proof by induction is the formal method — this is the same technique DP AA HL examiners test.

Level 5–6
Correct general rule. Verified with new cases. Justification present but may be informal or rely on examples rather than reasoning.
Level 7–8
Correct general rule. Rigorously verified. Justification is formal — algebraic, deductive, or by induction (Further). Explains why the rule must be true, not just that it is.
C
CommunicatingPrecision in language, notation, and structure

Grade 10 Crit C is assessed across all four units and expects professional mathematical communication. Logarithm notation, vector notation, sigma notation, set notation — each has precise conventions that must be followed.

The standard question: "Could an external examiner read this solution and follow every step without asking you a question?" If the answer is no — if you skipped steps, used ambiguous notation, or forgot units — those are Crit C marks lost.

This is also direct DP preparation. DP mark schemes award "method marks" only when working is shown clearly. A correct answer with no working gets zero in many DP questions.

Level 5–6
Correct notation most of the time. Working is logical and mostly complete. Diagrams present and labelled. Conclusions reference context.
Level 7–8
Precise notation throughout. Complete working with no unexplained jumps. All diagrams fully labelled. Conclusions mathematically precise and contextually grounded.
D
Applying in Real LifeModel, interpret, and evaluate critically

Grade 10 Crit D is the closest you get to a DP Internal Assessment before Year 11. The Finance Matters and Statistics units both require you to build a model, apply it to real data, and evaluate its validity.

At Level 7–8, your evaluation should address: What did you assume? How accurate is the model? What are the limitations? What would you change? These questions are the same five IA criteria in different language.

A student who writes strong Crit D reflections in Grade 10 is practising exactly the thinking DP IA examiners reward at Criterion E (Evaluation) and Criterion D (Reflection).

Level 5–6
Applies correct mathematics to a real context. Interprets results in terms of the situation. Identifies assumptions and at least one limitation.
Level 7–8
Builds a complete model with explicitly stated assumptions. Interprets all results precisely in context. Evaluates the model critically — addresses accuracy, limitations, and improvements with mathematical specificity.

Skills Bank

SequencesAll streams

Arithmetic & Geometric Sequences

Arithmetic: common difference d. General term: uₙ = u₁ + (n−1)d. Geometric: common ratio r. General term: uₙ = u₁ × rⁿ⁻¹.

Worked example
Geometric: u₁ = 3, r = 2 → u₅ = 3 × 2⁴ = 48
Arithmetic: u₁ = 5, d = 3 → u₁₀ = 5 + 9×3 = 32
SequencesExtended+

Sum of a Geometric Series to Infinity

S∞ = u₁ / (1−r) when |r| < 1 (converging). If |r| ≥ 1 the series diverges and has no finite sum.

Worked example
u₁ = 10, r = 0.5: S∞ = 10 / (1−0.5) = 20
Check: |0.5| < 1 ✓ converges
SequencesExtended+

Logarithms & Exponential Equations

Key log laws: log(ab) = log a + log b, log(a/b) = log a − log b, log(aⁿ) = n log a. To solve equations with different bases, take logs of both sides.

Worked example
Solve 3ˣ = 20: x log 3 = log 20
x = log 20 / log 3 ≈ 2.73
SequencesFurther

Proof by Induction

Three steps: (1) Base case — prove true for n=1. (2) Inductive step — assume true for n=k, prove true for n=k+1. (3) Conclude by the principle of mathematical induction.

Structure
"Assume true for n = k: [statement].
Show true for n = k+1: [algebra].
Therefore true for all n ∈ ℤ⁺ by PMI."
FunctionsAll streams

Function Transformations

f(x) + a shifts up, f(x+a) shifts left, af(x) stretches vertically, f(ax) compresses horizontally. Order matters when applying multiple transformations.

Quick reference
y = 2f(x−3) + 1: right 3, stretch ×2 vertically, up 1
FunctionsAll streams

Exponential Functions

y = aˣ: always positive, passes through (0,1), has horizontal asymptote at y = 0. Growth when a > 1, decay when 0 < a < 1.

Key features
y = 2ˣ: passes (0,1), (1,2), (2,4) — asymptote y = 0
y = (0.5)ˣ: same but decreasing — asymptote y = 0
FunctionsExtended+

Absolute Value Functions

|x| = x when x ≥ 0, and −x when x < 0. The graph forms a V-shape. To solve |f(x)| = k, solve f(x) = k and f(x) = −k separately.

Worked example
Solve |2x−3| = 5:
2x−3 = 5 → x = 4, or 2x−3 = −5 → x = −1
FunctionsFurther

Rational Functions & Sign Diagrams

Rational functions have vertical asymptotes where the denominator = 0 and horizontal asymptotes determined by degree comparison. Sign diagrams show where expressions are positive or negative.

Key skill
For (x−2)/(x+1) > 0: critical values x = 2, x = −1
Sign diagram → solution: x < −1 or x > 2
StatisticsAll streams

Standard Deviation

Measures spread around the mean. Use technology to calculate. Population σ uses n; sample s uses n−1. A larger standard deviation means data is more spread out.

Interpreting
σ = 2 → most data within 2 of the mean
σ = 15 → data much more spread out
StatisticsAll streams

Cumulative Frequency & Box Plots

Cumulative frequency graphs let you estimate median (50th percentile), Q1 (25th), Q3 (75th), and IQR. Box plots display the five-number summary visually.

Five-number summary
Min · Q1 · Median · Q3 · Max
IQR = Q3 − Q1 · Outlier if > Q3 + 1.5×IQR
StatisticsFurther

Normal & Skewed Distributions

Normal distribution: bell-shaped, symmetric, mean = median = mode. Positive skew: tail to right, mean > median. Negative skew: tail to left, mean < median. Quantify skewness to describe formally.

Quick test
Mean > Median → positive skew (tail right)
Mean < Median → negative skew (tail left)
TrigonometryAll streams

Sine Rule, Cosine Rule & Area

Sine rule: a/sin A = b/sin B. Cosine rule: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A. Area: ½ab sin C. Use sine rule for angle-side pairs; cosine rule for two sides and included angle or all three sides.

Which rule?
Know 2 sides + included angle → cosine rule
Know 2 angles + 1 side → sine rule
Know 2 sides + included angle → area = ½ab sin C
TrigonometryExtended+

Trig Graphs & Transformations

y = a sin(bx + c) + d: amplitude |a|, period 2π/b, horizontal shift −c/b, vertical shift d. These transformations map directly to DP Topic 3.

Worked example
y = 3 sin(2x) + 1:
Amplitude 3 · Period π · Midline y = 1
TrigonometryFurther

Double Angle Identities

sin 2A = 2 sin A cos A. cos 2A = cos²A − sin²A = 2cos²A − 1 = 1 − 2sin²A. Use to simplify expressions and solve equations that can't be solved directly.

Worked example
Simplify 2 sin 30° cos 30°:
= sin(2×30°) = sin 60° = √3/2
TrigonometryFurther

Vectors

Vectors have magnitude and direction. Written as column vectors or with i/j notation. Add by adding components; multiply by scalar by scaling each component. Magnitude: |v| = √(x²+y²).

Worked example
v = (3,4): |v| = √(9+16) = 5
v + w = (3,4)+(1,−2) = (4,2)

DP Bridge

Grade 10 is the last stop before the IB Diploma. Here's exactly where you're heading — and how your MYP work has been preparing you since Grade 7.

Which DP course fits your stream?

From: Standard (typically)

Applied Mathematics

ISHCMC's own practical maths course. Foundational and applications-focused. Non-IB pathway.

From: Standard or Extended

Math AI SL

Statistics, modelling, financial maths, and technology-driven problem solving. Ideal for students who engaged well with the real-life units.

From: Extended (typically)

Math AA SL

Functions, algebra, trigonometry, calculus, and proof. For students who enjoyed the structure and precision of Extended maths.

From: Extended or Further

Math AI HL

Extended statistical inference, complex modelling, and calculus applications. Requires strong Extended foundation.

From: Further (strongly recommended)

Math AA HL

The most rigorous IB mathematics course. Proof, complex numbers, vectors, differential equations. Further students are the primary pipeline.
