Grade 6 Mathematics · MYP Year 1

Where it all begins.

Number patterns, geometry, data, and fractions. Your first year of MYP Mathematics builds the foundations that every future unit — and every DP course — depends on.

"Every expert was once a beginner. Every pattern was once a mystery."

👋 Welcome to MYP Mathematics. Grade 6 is where you learn to think like a mathematician — not just to calculate, but to notice patterns, ask questions, and explain your reasoning. Every skill you build this year will reappear, in harder forms, across every year that follows.

Your Units

Five units across the year — including one interdisciplinary unit (IDU) shared with another subject. No streaming in Grade 6.

Unit 1 Relationships

How to Think Mathematically

Exploring patterns can help our understanding of relationships and support the categorising of objects.

ABCD
  • Identify and describe patterns in sets of whole numbers; identify square numbers, odds, evens, and prime numbers
  • Find the Highest Common Factor (HCF) and Least Common Multiple (LCM) of a set of whole numbers; express numbers as a product of prime factors using exponent form
  • Perform long multiplication and long division with whole numbers; apply order of operations (PEDMAS) to evaluate numerical expressions
  • Represent integers on a number line; calculate with integers (+, −, ×, ÷); understand and use absolute value; write inequality statements
  • Plot and identify coordinates in four quadrants
  • Extension
    Use prime factor decomposition to solve HCF and LCM problems
  • Extension
    Perform long multiplication and long division with decimal numbers

Prime factorisation and HCF/LCM underpin fraction operations in Unit 4. Integer operations and coordinate geometry are used in every algebraic unit from Grade 7 onward. Order of operations (PEDMAS) is tested in every DP paper — getting it right in Grade 6 saves marks for the next six years.

Criteria Guide

In Grade 6, you're meeting the MYP criteria for the first time. Here's what each one actually asks you to do — and how to move from good to excellent.

A note for Grade 6: You are assessed on all four criteria this year, but not every criterion is assessed in every unit — the dots on each unit card show which criteria apply. In Grade 6, the most important habit to build is showing your working clearly. A correct answer with no working shown can score zero on Criteria A, B, and C.
A

Knowing & Understanding

Know it, select it, apply it

Criterion A tests whether you can correctly select and apply mathematical knowledge. In Grade 6, this means knowing your number rules, angle facts, and fraction operations accurately — and applying them to questions you might not have seen before.

The key word at Level 7–8 is unfamiliar. A question that combines two things you know separately — like using prime factorisation to simplify a fraction — is unfamiliar. Can you still apply what you know?

Common mistake: Students who memorise procedures but can't adapt them to new contexts score Level 3–4. Students who understand why a rule works can apply it flexibly.

Level 5–6
Correctly applies mathematics to familiar problems. Some errors on more complex questions. Method is mostly clear.
Level 7–8
Accurately applies mathematics to both familiar and unfamiliar problems. Method is always clear. Answer includes correct units and is expressed appropriately.
B

Investigating Patterns

Notice, describe, generalise

Criterion B is about mathematical curiosity. You're given some examples, and you have to figure out what's happening — and then describe it in a way that works for all cases, not just the ones you've seen.

In Grade 6, Crit B appears in Unit 1 (number patterns) and Unit 2 (geometric patterns). The progression is: notice → describe → generalise → justify. Most Grade 6 students get the first two. The jump to Level 7–8 requires a genuine attempt at a general rule.

Example: Noticing that "the sum of two odd numbers is even" is Level 3–4. Writing a general rule — "if two numbers are both odd, their sum is always even" — and explaining why (an odd number is 2k+1, so their sum is 2k+2m+2 = 2(k+m+1), which is always even) is Level 7–8.

Level 5–6
Pattern identified and described clearly. A general rule is stated. Verified with at least one new example.
Level 7–8
Pattern described precisely. General rule stated correctly. Verified with multiple examples. Justification attempted — explains why the pattern works, not just that it does.
C

Communicating

Write mathematics, not just answers

Criterion C is about how you express your mathematical thinking. In Grade 6, this is where many students lose marks unnecessarily — not because their mathematics is wrong, but because it isn't communicated clearly.

Three non-negotiables for every piece of work assessed on Crit C:

1. Show all working. Don't skip steps. Each line should follow logically from the previous one.

2. Label everything. Graphs need axes labelled with variables and units. Diagrams need measurements marked. Tables need headings.

3. State your conclusion. Don't end with a number — end with a sentence that answers the question in context.

Level 5–6
Working is mostly present. Some labels on diagrams. Conclusions present but may be incomplete or lack context.
Level 7–8
All working shown with no unexplained jumps. All diagrams fully labelled. Conclusions mathematically precise and expressed in the context of the problem.
D

Applying in Real Life

Mathematics that means something

Criterion D is about using mathematics to make sense of the real world. In Grade 6, this appears most clearly in Units 3 and 4 — managing data to make decisions, understanding equity through percentages and ratios.

The four things Crit D always asks you to do: identify the mathematical problem in a real situation, select and apply appropriate mathematics, interpret your answer in context (not just write a number), and reflect on what the result means and whether it makes sense.

In Grade 6, focus on interpretation. After you calculate, ask yourself: "What does this number actually mean for the situation?" That sentence is often the difference between Level 5 and Level 7.

Level 5–6
Applies correct mathematics to the real-life context. Interprets the answer in context. Solution is mostly complete.
Level 7–8
Identifies the correct mathematical structure of the problem. Applies and completes the model. Interprets results precisely in context. Identifies at least one assumption or limitation.

Skills Bank

Number

Prime Factor Decomposition

Every whole number can be written as a product of primes. Use a factor tree to break the number down until all branches are prime. Write using exponent form.

Worked example
72 = 8 × 9 = 2³ × 3²
HCF(72, 48): 72=2³×3², 48=2⁴×3
HCF = 2³×3 = 24
Number

Order of Operations (PEDMAS)

Parentheses → Exponents → Division/Multiplication (left to right) → Addition/Subtraction (left to right). Never add before you multiply.

Worked example
3 + 4 × 2² − (6 ÷ 3)
= 3 + 4 × 4 − 2
= 3 + 16 − 2 = 17
Number

Integer Operations

Adding a negative = subtracting. Two negatives multiplied = positive. Absolute value |x| = distance from zero — always positive. −|−5| = −5, but |−5| = 5.

Quick rules
(−3) × (−4) = +12
(−3) × (+4) = −12
|−7| = 7 · −|−7| = −7
Number

Inequality Statements

> means greater than, < means less than, ≥ means greater than or equal to, ≤ means less than or equal to. The arrow on the number line points in the direction of solutions.

Reading inequalities
x > 3: all numbers to the right of 3 (open circle at 3)
x ≤ −1: all numbers left of −1 (closed circle at −1)
Geometry

Angle Types & Rules

Complementary: sum = 90°. Supplementary: sum = 180°. Angles on a straight line = 180°. Angles at a point = 360°. Vertically opposite angles are always equal.

Finding a missing angle
Supplementary pair: one angle = 65°
Other = 180° − 65° = 115°
Geometry

Triangles & Angle Sum

All triangle interior angles sum to 180°. Equilateral: three equal angles (60° each). Isosceles: two equal angles. Right-angled: one angle = 90°. The largest angle is always opposite the longest side.

Finding a missing angle
Triangle with angles 40° and 75°:
Third angle = 180° − 40° − 75° = 65°
Geometry

Area & Perimeter

Rectangle: A = l × w. Triangle: A = ½ × b × h. Composite shapes: split into simpler shapes, add areas. Perimeter = total distance around the outside. Always include units.

Composite shape
L-shape = large rectangle − small rectangle
Or split into two separate rectangles and add.
Geometry

Volume & Surface Area of Cuboids

Volume = l × w × h (units³). Surface area = sum of all six face areas = 2(lw + lh + wh) (units²). Nets show all faces unfolded flat — great for visualising surface area.

Worked example
Cuboid 3cm × 4cm × 5cm:
V = 60 cm³
SA = 2(12+15+20) = 94 cm²
Data & Probability

Types of Data

Quantitative data is numerical (discrete = countable, continuous = measurable). Qualitative data is categorical (nominal = no order, ordinal = has order). Primary = collected by you; secondary = collected by someone else.

Quick examples
Discrete: number of students (can't have 2.5)
Continuous: height in cm (can be any value)
Ordinal: satisfaction ratings (1–5 in order)
Data & Probability

Mean, Median, Mode & Range

Mean = sum ÷ count. Median = middle value when ordered. Mode = most frequent value. Range = max − min. Median and mode are better measures when there are extreme outliers.

Worked example
Data: 3, 7, 7, 9, 14
Mean = 40÷5 = 8 · Median = 7
Mode = 7 · Range = 14−3 = 11
Data & Probability

Probability Basics

Theoretical probability = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes. Relative frequency = number of times it happened ÷ total trials. As trials increase, relative frequency approaches theoretical probability.

Worked example
Rolled a die 60 times, got 6 exactly 12 times.
Relative frequency = 12/60 = 0.2
Theoretical P(6) = 1/6 ≈ 0.167
Fractions & Rationals

Converting: Fractions, Decimals, Percentages

Fraction → decimal: divide numerator by denominator. Decimal → percentage: multiply by 100. Percentage → fraction: write over 100 and simplify. Memorise common ones: ½=0.5=50%, ¼=0.25=25%, ⅓≈0.333=33.3%.

Quick conversions
3/8 = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375 = 37.5%
0.64 = 64/100 = 16/25
Fractions & Rationals

Dividing Fractions

To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal (flip it). Keep the first fraction, Change the ÷ to ×, Flip the second fraction. (KCF)

Worked example
3/4 ÷ 2/5 = 3/4 × 5/2 = 15/8 = 1⅞
Always simplify your final answer.
Fractions & Rationals

Rational Numbers on a Number Line

Rational numbers are any number that can be written as a fraction p/q where q ≠ 0. To order fractions, convert to a common denominator or to decimals, then place on the number line.

Ordering
Order: 2/3, 3/4, 5/8
→ 16/24, 18/24, 15/24
→ 5/8 < 2/3 < 3/4
Fractions & Rationals

Terminating vs Non-Terminating Decimals

Terminating: the decimal ends (e.g. 1/4 = 0.25). Non-terminating recurring: the decimal repeats forever (e.g. 1/3 = 0.333…). A fraction terminates only if its denominator (in lowest terms) has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.

Quick test
1/8 → denominator = 2³ → terminates (0.125)
1/6 → denominator = 2×3 → recurring (0.1666…)
