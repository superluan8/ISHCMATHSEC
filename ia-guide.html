Finding your topic

Most students pick a topic they like. That's necessary but not sufficient. The real question is whether your topic can sustain 12–20 pages of genuine mathematical investigation.

The three-question filter

1. Do I genuinely care about this?

Not "it's interesting." Real curiosity — something you've wondered about before maths class suggested it. Examiners read hundreds of IAs. Performed enthusiasm is obvious. Genuine curiosity is not.

2. Does the mathematics have at least two layers?

Layer 1: the surface finding. Layer 2: a deeper extension, generalisation, or unexpected connection. Topics with only one layer run out at 8 pages and force padding. Two layers give you a natural investigation arc.

3. Is there a concrete anchor?

A real dataset, a measurable real-world object, a historical result to verify. Abstract investigations that live entirely in symbolic manipulation struggle to demonstrate personal engagement — there's nothing to return to.

4. Can I drive this myself?

If your investigation exists as a tutorial online and you'd be replicating it step-by-step, the examiner will know. Your topic should require you to make genuine decisions about what to investigate next.

The three failure modes

1

Too thin

Interesting topic, but the mathematics runs out after 6 pages. Classic example: "the golden ratio in architecture." Compelling premise, shallow mathematical depth. You end up measuring things and noting they approximate φ — that's not investigation, it's observation.

2

Too borrowed

The student finds a method online and replicates it without understanding. The work may be mathematically correct but personal engagement collapses — there are no genuine decisions, no surprises, no authentic voice. Examiners see this pattern constantly.

3

Too abstract

Mathematically rich, but the student can't explain why they care. Usually happens when a student chooses a topic to impress rather than to explore. Without genuine interest, reflection reads as hollow and personal engagement is impossible to demonstrate.

From curiosity to investigation question

A good investigation question is specific, mathematical, and open enough to surprise you. It should be answerable — but not obviously so.

❌ Too broad / not mathematical
"How does music relate to mathematics?"

"Is the stock market predictable?"

"What is the golden ratio?"
✓ Specific, mathematical, investigable
"To what extent does a sinusoidal model accurately represent the pitch variation in a Vietnamese tonal language?"

"How well does an exponential regression model the cumulative race lap times of a Formula 1 strategy switch?"

"What is the relationship between compound interest frequency and effective annual yield, and at what point does increasing frequency become negligible?"

A · 4 marksPresentation

Is the exploration well-structured, with a clear title, introduction, and conclusion? Does it read as a coherent mathematical narrative rather than a collection of calculations?

0–1
No clear structure. The exploration reads as a list of calculations. No introduction or conclusion. Title present but uninformative.
2–3
Introduction and conclusion present. Sections are identifiable. Some flow between ideas. Minor structural gaps or abrupt transitions.
4
The exploration reads as a coherent piece of mathematical writing. Each section connects to the next. Introduction sets up the question clearly. Conclusion answers it with reference to the investigation.

What "coherent narrative" actually means

A reader who knows the topic should be able to read your IA and understand: why you investigated this, what you did, what you found, and what you concluded — without asking you a single question.

This means every graph, table, and calculation is introduced before it appears and referred to after. You never drop a graph in and move on without explaining what it shows and why it matters to your investigation.

The required structure

Introduction

State your topic, why you chose it, what your investigation question is, and what mathematical approach you'll take. Should be 1–2 pages maximum. Does not need to be long — it needs to be purposeful.

Body

The investigation itself. Each section should have a clear mathematical purpose. Calculations, graphs, and tables are embedded in prose — not just dumped. Transitions between sections explain why you're moving to the next stage.

Conclusion

Answers your investigation question directly. Connects to what you found. Does not introduce new mathematics — this is reflection and synthesis. Should reference specific results from your investigation, not just restate the method.

❌ Weak presentation
"Figure 3 shows the regression line. The R² value is 0.87. Next, I will look at residuals."

The graph appears with no explanation of what it shows, why 0.87 matters, or how this connects to the investigation question.
✓ Strong presentation
"Figure 3 shows the exponential regression fitted to my data. The R² value of 0.87 suggests a reasonably strong fit, though not perfect — this led me to examine the residuals in the next section to investigate whether the deviations are systematic or random, which would indicate whether a different model might be more appropriate."

B · 4 marksMathematical Communication

Is mathematical language used correctly and precisely throughout? Are graphs fully labelled? Is notation consistent and appropriate to the level?

0–1
Significant notation errors. Graphs unlabelled or axes missing. Mathematical language used imprecisely or incorrectly throughout.
2–3
Mostly correct notation. Most graphs labelled. Some inconsistencies in notation or language. Working is generally clear but has gaps.
4
Precise, consistent notation throughout. All graphs and tables fully labelled with titles, axis labels, and units. Mathematical language used correctly. Every claim is supported by working.

The examiner's test

Could an IB examiner who has never met you read your IA and follow every step of your reasoning without needing to ask for clarification?

In DP examinations, a correct answer with no working shown typically receives zero marks. The same principle applies here. The mathematics must be communicated, not just performed.

Common communication errors that cost marks

Graphs without titles or axis labels

Every graph must have a descriptive title, both axes labelled with variable names and units, and a consistent scale. A graph labelled only "Graph 1" with unlabelled axes loses Crit B marks every time.

Inconsistent notation

Switching between f(x) and y= in the same exploration, using × and * interchangeably, or defining a variable as n in one section and k in another. Pick conventions and hold them for the entire IA.

Unsupported claims

"The correlation is strong" — says who? Show the Pearson r value. "The model fits well" — your R² of 0.94 demonstrates this. Claims must be backed by specific mathematical evidence.

Rounding without acknowledgement

If you round intermediate values, state your rounding convention early and apply it consistently. "I rounded all values to 3 significant figures throughout to match the precision of my measured data."

Technology screenshots without explanation

A GDC or Desmos screenshot is not a substitute for showing key steps. Use technology to confirm or visualise — but document the mathematical process separately.

Appendix overloading

Raw data, full calculation tables, and repetitive working belong in an appendix — but the key steps must appear in the body. Examiners are not required to read appendices.

❌ Weak communication
"I used the formula and got 0.83. This means there is correlation so the model works."

Which formula? 0.83 of what? What kind of correlation? How does this confirm the model?
✓ Strong communication
"Using technology, I calculated Pearson's correlation coefficient r = 0.83 for the linear regression of reaction time (ms) against caffeine dose (mg). This value indicates a moderately strong positive linear relationship, suggesting that my linear model captures a meaningful proportion of the variation in the data, though the remaining 31% (1 − r²) suggests other variables may also be influential."

C · 3 marksPersonal Engagement

The most misunderstood criterion. Examiners are not looking for a paragraph saying you love your topic. They are looking for evidence inside the mathematics that you are genuinely driving the investigation.

0–1
The investigation follows a prescribed path with no evidence of personal decision-making. Could have been written by anyone using the same source material.
2
Some evidence of personal interest. The student makes some of their own decisions. At least one genuine moment of curiosity or surprise is visible in the work.
3
Authentic voice throughout. The student demonstrably chose their direction. Genuine engagement is visible in how they respond to results — following up surprises, changing approach, making connections to their own experience.

What engagement looks like inside the maths

✓ Asking your own follow-up questions — "This result surprised me. I expected [X] but found [Y], so I decided to also investigate whether..."

✓ Making genuine decisions — "I initially planned to use linear regression, but after examining the scatter plot, I decided to test an exponential model instead because..."

✓ Connecting to your own context — "As someone who has raced karts, I was curious whether the optimal overtaking strategy in F1 is consistent with what I've observed at [track]..."

✓ Showing dead ends — A student who tried something that didn't work and explains why demonstrates more genuine engagement than one with a suspiciously perfect path.

The iterative process and personal engagement

An IA that reads as written in a single sitting scores low on Criterion C. An IA that shows the student changed direction, extended their scope, or revisited an earlier result based on a later finding reads as genuinely personal.

This is why the iterative process matters for Criterion C specifically. Each draft cycle is an opportunity to discover something that genuinely surprises you — and respond to it in the writing. That response is personal engagement made visible.

❌ Performed engagement (scores 0–1)
"I chose this topic because I am passionate about Formula 1 racing and have always been fascinated by the mathematics behind it. This topic is personally meaningful to me."

This is in the introduction and never appears again. The investigation itself follows a textbook method with no evidence of the student's own thinking.
✓ Genuine engagement (scores 2–3)
"When I fitted the linear model to my first dataset, the residual plot showed a clear pattern — residuals increased with x — suggesting my linear assumption was wrong. Rather than forcing a better-looking R² by removing outliers as I initially planned, I re-examined the raw data and noticed the relationship looked curved. This led me to test a quadratic model, which I hadn't originally planned to include."

Questions to unlock genuine engagement in your writing

What result surprised you most?

Write about it as a surprise. What did you expect? What did you find? What did that make you investigate next?

When did you change your plan?

Every good IA has at least one moment where the investigation changed direction. Make that visible. Explain what caused the change and what you decided to do instead.

What connection did you make that wasn't suggested to you?

Did something from your IA connect to another subject, a personal experience, or something you'd read? That connection — if it's genuine — is high-scoring engagement.

What would you investigate if you had more time?

A student who knows exactly what they'd explore next is demonstrating that they've genuinely engaged with the limits of their own investigation. This is also Criterion D material.

D · 3 marksReflection

Reflection is not a concluding paragraph. It is an ongoing process throughout the exploration. The difference between a 2 and a 3 on this criterion is almost always about where reflection appears — and how mathematically specific it is — not just whether it appears.

0–1
Little or no reflection present. Any reflection that exists is superficial ("this was an interesting investigation") or appears only at the very end.
2
Reflection is present, mostly at the end. The student identifies limitations but doesn't connect them to specific mathematical consequences. Some reflection on the process is evident.
3
Reflection appears throughout — in the body, not just the conclusion. The student questions assumptions while using them. Limitations are connected to specific mathematical consequences. The conclusion proposes meaningful extensions.

Where to embed reflection

While choosing a method: "I chose Pearson's r here, which assumes a linear relationship. If the relationship were non-linear, this coefficient would underestimate the true correlation strength."

When a result is unexpected: "This value was lower than predicted. I considered whether this was due to the sample size (n=12) introducing significant sampling error, or whether my exponential model was misspecified."

At the conclusion: Revisit your original question. Answer it with specific reference to your results. Then ask: what would the next investigation look like?

The reflection depth spectrum

These four levels apply to the same limitation — "my sample size was small." Notice how depth increases.

Level 1 — Superficial (scores 0–1)
"My sample size was small, which is a limitation."
Level 2 — Identified (scores 2)
"My sample size of n=12 is a limitation. A larger sample would produce more reliable results."
Level 3 — Mathematically grounded (scores 3)
"My sample size of n=12 is a significant limitation. Using the Fisher z-transformation, the 95% confidence interval for my r=0.84 spans approximately [0.50, 0.96], meaning I cannot rule out the possibility that the true relationship is only moderately strong. To narrow this interval to ±0.10, I would need approximately n=85 observations — a meaningful target for a follow-up investigation with more access to race timing data."

E · 6 marksUse of Mathematics

The gatekeeper criterion. Criterion E is assessed relative to your course level. What counts as "sophisticated" for AI SL is different from AA HL. But the principle is the same: the mathematics must be correct, relevant, and demonstrate genuine understanding — not just the ability to execute a procedure.

0–1
Mostly incorrect mathematics. Procedures applied without understanding. Errors are significant and affect the conclusions.
2
Some correct mathematics. But limited in scope or sophistication relative to the course level. Mostly procedural with little evidence of understanding.
3
Correct and appropriately complex mathematics for the course level. Some evidence that the student understands what they're doing, not just how to do it.
4
Correct, well-chosen, and sufficiently sophisticated mathematics. The student clearly understands the mathematics being used and makes appropriate choices.
5
Correct, well-chosen, and sufficiently sophisticated. The student clearly understands the mathematics and makes appropriate choices. Evidence of mathematical insight beyond procedure.
6
Correct, sophisticated, and precise. The mathematics drives the exploration rather than supporting it. The student demonstrates mastery — making connections, identifying edge cases, choosing methods with evident understanding of alternatives. For HL: rigour and elegance are evident.

Why Criterion E is the gatekeeper

A perfect score on A–D — presentation (4), communication (4), engagement (3), reflection (3) — gives you 14 out of 20. To reach 17–20, you need E=3 or higher. There is no path to a top score without strong mathematics.

If your mathematics is only worth E=2, your ceiling is 16 regardless of how well-written your IA is. This is why topic choice matters so much — you need a question rich enough to sustain mathematics that is correct, relevant, and sophisticated for your level.

Procedural vs. conceptual

Procedural: Substituting values into a formula, reading a regression output from a calculator, applying a rule that was taught.

Conceptual: Explaining why the formula applies, interpreting what the regression output means for your specific context, making a judgment about which method to use and why it's more appropriate than alternatives.

Sophistication by course level

Course Minimum sophistication for E=3–4 What pushes to E=5–6
AI SL E=3–4: Correct statistical analysis — regression, Pearson r, standard deviation — with appropriate model selection and meaningful interpretation of technology outputs in context. The student understands what the outputs mean, not just how to produce them. E=5–6: Multiple statistical tools used in combination and compared analytically. Student justifies why one model is more appropriate than another using mathematical reasoning (e.g. comparing R² and residual patterns across models). Uncertainty is quantified. At E=6, results are interpreted with precision and the student demonstrates awareness of the conditions under which the chosen method is valid.
AA SL E=3–4: Correct use of AA SL content — functions, differentiation, integration, or trigonometry — applied analytically, not just via GDC. At least one technique that goes beyond substitution into a given formula. The student shows they understand the mathematical structure, not just the procedure. E=5–6: Derivations or proofs of key results used in the exploration. Connections between topic areas (e.g. using calculus to find an optimum that feeds a probability model). Non-routine problem solved analytically with full justification. At E=6, the mathematics is self-directed — the student is not applying a taught template but constructing an argument.
AI HL E=3–4: Statistical inference correctly applied — hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, or complex modelling — with evidence that the student understands the conditions under which each method is valid (e.g. checking normality before a t-test, stating H₀ and H₁ clearly). E=5–6: Markov chains, transition matrices, Poisson or binomial distributions used in a genuinely investigative — not template — way. Confidence intervals constructed and interpreted with mathematical justification for the chosen level of significance. At E=6, the student is making original decisions about analytical approach and demonstrating that the HL extension content is genuinely understood and applied with rigour.
AA HL E=3–4: Correct use of HL content — complex numbers, vectors, differential equations, proof by induction, or series — where genuinely relevant to the investigation. The mathematics is substantially beyond AA SL in scope and technique. E=5–6: Original proof or derivation that the student constructs rather than recalls. Use of HL techniques (e.g. Maclaurin series, vector cross products, second-order differential equations) to answer a non-standard question. At E=6, mathematical elegance and rigour are both present — the student anticipates edge cases, states domains and conditions, and justifies every claim. ❌ Procedural (scores E=2)
"I entered the data into my GDC and obtained the regression line y = 2.3x + 14.1 with R² = 0.91. This shows the model fits the data well."

Correct, but procedural. No evidence of understanding what R² means, why linear regression was chosen, or whether the assumptions of the model are met.
✓ Conceptual (scores E=4–6 depending on course)
"Before applying linear regression, I verified two key assumptions: that the relationship appeared linear on the scatter plot, and that the residuals were approximately normally distributed. Having confirmed both (Figure 4 and Figure 5), I fitted the model y = 2.3x + 14.1. The R² = 0.91 indicates that 91% of the variance in [y] is explained by [x], though R² alone cannot confirm causation — other lurking variables may be influential, which I explore in Section 4."

The iterative process

A 17–20 IA is not written in one sitting. It is built across multiple cycles of investigation, reflection, and revision. Here is what each stage should produce.

1

The exploration draft — write to find out

Weeks 1–2Crit CCrit E

Don't write an IA. Investigate. Follow your curiosity. Try things. Let the mathematics lead. If a result surprises you, follow it. If something doesn't work, note why and try differently. The goal of this stage is not a draft — it's genuine mathematical experience that becomes the raw material of your IA.

What you should have at the end of this stage: A collection of findings, graphs, calculations, and dead ends. At least one result that surprised you. A sense of what the most interesting part of your investigation is — which may be different from what you started with.

2

The structure draft — shape it as an exploration

Weeks 3–4Crit ACrit BCrit D

Now write the IA. Build the narrative arc. Introduce your investigation question. Show your mathematical work in logical sequence. Critically: identify where genuine engagement happened in your exploration and make it visible in the writing. Add reflection at the key turning points — not just the conclusion. Every graph gets a title and labels. Every claim gets evidence.

What you should have at the end of this stage: A complete draft, 12–20 pages, with introduction, body, and conclusion. All five criteria are addressed. The writing has your voice. Reflection is woven through the body, not just at the end.

3

The precision draft — eliminate every communication error

Weeks 5–6Crit ACrit B

Read your IA as an examiner who has never met you. Does every graph have a title and labelled axes? Is every notation consistent from first use to last? Is every claim backed by a specific result? Are all equations formatted correctly? Does the introduction set up exactly what the conclusion answers? This is the stage that turns a 14 into a 17.

What you should have at the end of this stage: A submission-ready exploration where every page has been examined with a critical eye. Use the Final Checklist (last tab) as your exit criteria.

The feedback loop — how to use teacher feedback at each stage

After draft 1: Ask about the mathematics

"Is the mathematics I've chosen rich enough for the course level? Is there a more interesting direction I haven't considered?"

After draft 2: Ask about structure and reflection

"Does my reflection feel genuine and mathematical? Is the narrative clear? Can you follow the investigation without asking me questions?"

After draft 3: Ask about communication precision

"Are there any notation inconsistencies or unlabelled graphs I've missed? Is every claim supported by evidence in the text?"
