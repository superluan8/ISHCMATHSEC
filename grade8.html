Criteria Guide

What A, B, C, and D actually mean in Grade 8 — with examples from your units, not just rubric language.

A
Knowing & Understanding Select and apply mathematical knowledge

This is the "can you do the maths?" criterion. In Grade 8, this mostly means solving equations, drawing graphs, applying Pythagoras, and working with quadratics correctly.

A correct answer alone isn't enough. You need to show which method you used and why it works. A right answer with no working can score 0 on Crit A.

Common mark losses: right answer, wrong method shown; arithmetic slip in the final step; using a valid but less efficient approach when a better one was taught.

Level 5–6
Solves familiar problems correctly most of the time. Method is shown but may have gaps. Selects appropriate techniques with some guidance.
Level 7–8
Solves both familiar and unfamiliar problems with full, clear working. Selects the most efficient method and reflects on whether the solution makes sense.
B
Investigating Patterns Observe, generalise, and justify

Crit B is about mathematical thinking, not just answers. In Grade 8 this appears in Pack it In (3D pattern generalisation) and Art of Maths 2 (quadratic sequences).

The key stages: Notice → Organise → Describe → Generalise → Verify → Justify. Most students stop at "describe." Level 7–8 requires you to write a general rule and explain why it works for any case, not just check it for three examples.

Important: Crit B tasks have no single right answer. The quality of your investigation matters more than what pattern you find.

Level 5–6
Identifies a pattern and describes it clearly. Writes a general rule. Tests the rule with at least one new case.
Level 7–8
Writes a correct general rule, verifies it with cases not used to find the pattern, and provides a mathematical reason for why the rule works (not just "it fits").
C
Communicating Use mathematical language and notation

The most underrated criterion. Students drop marks here without understanding why — because the maths was right but the communication was unclear or incomplete.

In Grade 8, Crit C means: correct notation for equations, graphs fully labelled (axes, scale, intercepts), working written in logical steps, and conclusions written in sentences that connect maths to the situation.

Ask yourself: "Would a classmate who missed this lesson understand my solution?" If yes, you're probably at Level 6+.

Level 5–6
Uses correct notation most of the time. Graphs are labelled. Working is mostly logical. Conclusions reference the context.
Level 7–8
Precise notation throughout. All diagrams fully labelled. Working flows logically with clear reasoning at each step. Conclusions are precise and mathematically justified.
D
Applying in Real Life Model real situations mathematically

Crit D asks you to move between the real world and mathematics — in both directions. In Grade 8 this appears in Pack it In (3D volumes and real packaging) and Carbon Conundrum (real environmental data).

Four stages: Identify the situation → Build the model → Interpret results → Reflect. Most students interpret their results but never reflect. Reflection means asking whether your model was accurate, what you assumed, and what the limitations are.

"My answer was correct" is not reflection. "I assumed the containers were perfect cylinders, but real cans have seams which would reduce capacity slightly" — that is reflection.

Level 5–6
Applies appropriate mathematics to the situation. Interprets results in context. Mentions at least one limitation of the approach.
Level 7–8
Builds a complete model with stated assumptions. Interprets all results precisely in context. Reflects critically on accuracy, limitations, and possible improvements — with specific mathematical reference.

Algebra All streams

Solving Linear Equations

Rearrange and solve equations with unknowns on both sides, including those requiring expansion or factorisation first.

Worked example
Solve: 3(2x − 1) = 4x + 7
→ 6x − 3 = 4x + 7 → 2x = 10 → x = 5
Algebra All streams

Equation of a Line

Find the equation of a line given gradient and y-intercept, or a gradient and a point. Recognise parallel lines by comparing gradients.

Worked example
Gradient = 2, passes through (1, 5):
y − 5 = 2(x − 1) → y = 2x + 3
Algebra Extended+

Simultaneous Equations

Solve pairs of linear equations using elimination, substitution, and graphically. Identify when systems have no solution or infinite solutions.

Worked example
2x + y = 8 and x − y = 1
Add: 3x = 9 → x = 3, y = 2 → (3, 2)
Algebra All streams

Linear Inequalities

Solve inequalities and represent solutions on a number line and in interval notation. Remember: multiplying or dividing by a negative flips the inequality sign.

Worked example
Solve −2x + 3 > 7
→ −2x > 4 → x < −2 (sign flips!)
Geometry All streams

Pythagoras' Theorem

Find missing sides in right-angled triangles. Also applies to find distance between two points on a coordinate grid using the distance formula.

Worked example
Legs 3 and 4: c² = 3² + 4² = 25 → c = 5
Distance (1,2) to (4,6): √(9+16) = 5
Geometry All streams

Circles — Area & Circumference

Calculate area (πr²) and circumference (2πr) of circles. Extended and Further students also calculate arc lengths and sector areas.

Worked example
Radius = 5 cm:
Area = π(5²) ≈ 78.5 cm² · Circumference = 2π(5) ≈ 31.4 cm
Geometry All streams

Volume of 3D Solids

Calculate volume of cuboids, prisms, and cylinders. Further students extend to spheres, cones, and pyramids.

Worked example
Cylinder r = 3, h = 10:
V = π(3²)(10) = 90π ≈ 283 cm³
Geometry Extended+

Surds

Simplify, add, subtract, multiply, and divide surds. Rationalise denominators. Know that √2 is irrational.

Worked example
Simplify √50: √(25×2) = 5√2
Rationalise 1/√3: × √3/√3 = √3/3
Statistics All streams

Measures of Central Tendency & Spread

Calculate mean, median, mode, and IQR. Draw and interpret box-and-whisker plots. Identify and consider outliers.

Key rule
Outlier test: value < Q1 − 1.5×IQR or > Q3 + 1.5×IQR
IQR = Q3 − Q1
Statistics All streams

Scatter Plots & Correlation

Draw and describe scatter graphs. Identify direction and strength of correlation. Draw a line of best fit; use it to interpolate and extrapolate.

Key language
Strong positive · Weak negative · No correlation
Interpolation = within data range · Extrapolation = outside it
Statistics Extended+

Pearson's Correlation Coefficient

Use technology (GDC or Google Sheets) to calculate r. Interpret r: values close to ±1 indicate strong correlation, close to 0 indicate weak or no correlation.

Interpreting r
r = 0.92 → strong positive correlation
r = −0.15 → very weak negative correlation
Quadratics All streams

Expanding & Factorising

Expand single and double brackets. Factorise using common factor, difference of two squares, and trinomials. Standard works with a = 1; Extended and Further work with any value of a.

Worked example
Expand: (2x+3)(x−4) = 2x²−5x−12
Factorise: x²+5x+6 = (x+2)(x+3)
Quadratics All streams

Quadratic Formula

Solve any quadratic equation using x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a. Standard uses a calculator; Extended and Further simplify surds in the answer.

Worked example
x²+5x+3 = 0 → x = (−5 ± √13) / 2
≈ −0.70 or −4.30
Quadratics All streams

Sketching Quadratic Graphs

Draw a parabola by finding x-intercepts, y-intercept, and vertex. Vertex x-coordinate: x = −b/2a. Label all key points clearly.

Key steps
1. y-intercept: set x = 0
2. x-intercepts: factorise or use formula
3. Vertex: x = −b/2a, then find y
Quadratics Further

Completing the Square

Rewrite ax²+bx+c in the form a(x+h)²+k to find the vertex directly. Links to vertical and horizontal transformations of the parabola.

Worked example
x²+6x+5 = (x+3)²−4
→ Vertex at (−3, −4)
