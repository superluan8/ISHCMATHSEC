What Criterion B actually asks

The four verbs — observe, conjecture, verify, justify — are not the same thing. Most students collapse them into one step. Here's what each one demands.

Step 1 · Observe

Write down exactly what you notice

Be precise and mathematical. "It goes up" scores 0. "Each term increases by 3" is an observation. "The difference between consecutive terms is constant" is a better one.

Try it: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25... What do you notice? (Write more than one thing.)
Step 2 · Conjecture

State what you think is always true

A conjecture is a claim about all cases, not just the ones you've seen. It must be falsifiable — you should be able to imagine (even if you can't find) a counterexample.

Weak: "The next term is 36."
Strong: "The nth term is n², so all terms are perfect squares."
Step 3 · Verify

Test cases you haven't already seen

Verification means testing your conjecture on new examples — ones not used to form the conjecture. Five new cases that all work gives you confidence. But verification alone is never justification.

Remember: No number of examples proves a conjecture is always true. One counterexample disproves it entirely.
Step 4 · Justify

Explain why it must always hold

Justification answers the question: why can't there be a counterexample? It uses the mathematical structure of the pattern — algebra, geometry, logical argument — not more examples.

Ask yourself: If someone claimed my pattern breaks at n = 1000, could I prove them wrong without checking? If yes, you have a justification.

The most common mistake

Checking ten cases is not a justification.

It never has been, and it never will be. No matter how many cases you check, you cannot check infinity. The conjecture "the sum of two odd numbers is always even" has infinitely many cases. Checking 1+3, 5+7, and 11+13 proves nothing about 100,000,001 + 99,999,999.

The actual justification

Any odd number can be written as 2k + 1. Two odd numbers: (2a + 1) + (2b + 1) = 2a + 2b + 2 = 2(a + b + 1). Since 2(a + b + 1) is divisible by 2, it must be even — for every possible value of a and b, without exception.

Verify vs Justify

Same conjecture. Four different responses. Can you tell which is which — and which band each earns?

Pattern to investigate
When you add any two consecutive integers, the result is always odd.
e.g. 3 + 4 = 7    10 + 11 = 21    99 + 100 = 199

Verification only — no conjecture stated"I tried 1+2=3 (odd), 4+5=9 (odd), 7+8=15 (odd), 20+21=41 (odd). It works every time."
❌ No general claim. No reason why. More examples do not raise this score.
3–4
Conjecture stated, verified, no justification"I think the sum of any two consecutive integers is always odd. I checked: 2+3=5, 15+16=31, 100+101=201, 1000+1001=2001. All odd. My conjecture is correct."
⚠ Conjecture is clear and verified. But "I checked many cases" is still not justification.
5–6
Partial justification — reasoning present but incomplete"Consecutive integers are always one even and one odd. Even + odd = odd. So the sum must be odd."
✓ Good reasoning. Uses mathematical structure. To reach 7–8, explain why even + odd must always equal odd.

Full justification — structural, works for all cases"Let the smaller integer be n. Then the consecutive pair is n and n+1. Their sum is n + (n+1) = 2n + 1. Since 2n is always even (divisible by 2), adding 1 always gives an odd number. This holds for every integer n without exception."
✓ Uses algebra to represent all cases. Conclusion follows necessarily. Cannot be disproved.
Pattern to investigate · Geometric
The number of diagonals in a polygon seems to follow a pattern:
Triangle (3 sides): 0 diagonals    Square (4): 2    Pentagon (5): 5    Hexagon (6): 9
1–2
Observation without generalisation"Each time you add a side, you get more diagonals. A hexagon has 9 diagonals."
3–4
Conjecture with pattern rule, no justification"The differences are 2, 3, 4 — going up by 1 each time. So for n sides, the diagonals are 0, 2, 5, 9, 14... I predict a heptagon has 14. I checked by drawing — yes, 14."
5–6
Formula derived, partial reasoning"The formula is n(n−3)/2. I found this by looking at the pattern differences and checking it works for all my cases. A 10-gon would have 10×7/2 = 35 diagonals."
7–8
Formula derived from structure"In a polygon with n sides, each vertex connects to n−1 other vertices. Remove the 2 adjacent vertices (sides, not diagonals), leaving n−3 diagonals from each vertex. Total connections: n(n−3). Each diagonal is counted twice (once from each end), so total diagonals = n(n−3)/2. This formula is derived from the structure, not the pattern — it must hold for all n ≥ 3."

Grade 6 Unit 1 · How to Think Mathematically Number Patterns
Square numbers and their differences
The pattern
1² = 1
2² = 4    difference from previous: 3
3² = 9    difference from previous: 5
4² = 16   difference from previous: 7
5² = 25   difference from previous: 9
01 · Observe
The differences between consecutive square numbers are 3, 5, 7, 9... What type of numbers are these? What else do you notice about them?
02 · Conjecture
Complete this statement: "The difference between consecutive square numbers is always __________, and the differences form a __________ sequence."
03 · Verify
Check your conjecture for 6² and 7². Does the pattern hold? What is the difference between n² and (n+1)²?
04 · Justify
Can you write (n+1)² − n² and simplify it? What does your simplified expression tell you about why the differences must always be odd?
Bands 3–4 (Verified, conjecture clear)
The differences are 3, 5, 7, 9 — all odd numbers. I think the differences between consecutive square numbers are always odd. I checked: 6²=36, 7²=49, difference=13 (odd) ✓. 10²=100, 11²=121, difference=21 (odd) ✓. The differences also form an arithmetic sequence going up by 2 each time.
Bands 7–8 (Justified algebraically)
Let the two consecutive square numbers be n² and (n+1)². Their difference is (n+1)² − n² = n² + 2n + 1 − n² = 2n + 1. Since 2n is always even, 2n + 1 is always odd — regardless of the value of n. This proves the difference between any two consecutive square numbers is always odd. The differences also form an arithmetic sequence because 2n + 1 increases by exactly 2 each time n increases by 1.
Grade 7 Unit 4 · Sound Structures Geometry
Interior angles of regular polygons
The pattern
Equilateral triangle (3 sides): each interior angle = 60°
Square (4 sides): each interior angle = 90°
Regular pentagon (5 sides): each interior angle = 108°
Regular hexagon (6 sides): each interior angle = 120°
01 · Observe
What happens to the interior angle as the number of sides increases? How much does it increase each time?
02 · Conjecture
Write a formula for the interior angle of a regular polygon with n sides. Hint: what is the total of all interior angles first?
03 · Verify
Use your formula to predict the interior angle of a regular octagon (8 sides). Verify by checking the angle sum of an octagon.
04 · Justify
Any polygon can be divided into triangles from one vertex. How many triangles does an n-sided polygon produce? Use this to explain where your formula comes from — not just that it works.
Bands 3–4
Each time I add a side, the angle increases: 60°, 90°, 108°, 120°, 128.57°... The differences are 30°, 18°, 12°, 8.57° — decreasing. I predict the formula involves dividing by n. My conjecture: interior angle = (n−2) × 180° ÷ n. Check for octagon: (8−2)×180÷8 = 6×180÷8 = 135°.
Bands 7–8
From one vertex of an n-sided polygon, you can draw diagonals to create exactly n−2 triangles (you can't draw to adjacent vertices or to yourself). Each triangle has an angle sum of 180°, so the total interior angle sum of any n-sided polygon is (n−2) × 180°. Since the polygon is regular, all n angles are equal, so each interior angle = (n−2) × 180° ÷ n. This formula is derived from the triangle decomposition — it works for any n ≥ 3 because the decomposition always produces exactly n−2 triangles, regardless of the polygon's size or orientation.
Grade 8 Unit 1 · Art of Maths Sequences
Triangle numbers and their sums
The pattern
T₁ = 1
T₂ = 1 + 2 = 3
T₃ = 1 + 2 + 3 = 6
T₄ = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10
T₅ = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 15

What is a formula for Tₙ — the sum of the first n positive integers?
01 · Observe
Look at the values 1, 3, 6, 10, 15. What are the differences? What happens when you double each triangle number?
02 · Conjecture
State a formula for Tₙ. Test it for T₆ and T₁₀ before moving on.
03–04 · Verify & Justify
Gauss's method: write the sum forwards and backwards, then add them. What do you get? Use this to derive the formula — not just confirm it. Can you also draw a geometric justification using a rectangle made from two triangle arrangements?
Bands 7–8 · Two methods of justification
Algebraic (Gauss's method):
Let S = 1 + 2 + 3 + ... + n
Also S = n + (n−1) + (n−2) + ... + 1
Adding: 2S = (n+1) + (n+1) + ... + (n+1) [n times] = n(n+1)
Therefore S = n(n+1)/2 ✓

Geometric justification:
Arrange Tₙ dots in a triangle. Make a second identical triangle and rotate it 180°. The two triangles together form an n × (n+1) rectangle containing n(n+1) dots. Since each triangle has half the dots, Tₙ = n(n+1)/2. This works for every n — the rectangle always has dimensions n by n+1.
Grade 9 Unit 1 · The Shape of U Algebra
The difference of two squares
The pattern
5² − 3² = 25 − 9 = 16 = 8 × 2
7² − 5² = 49 − 25 = 24 = 12 × 2
10² − 6² = 100 − 36 = 64 = 16 × 4
9² − 4² = 81 − 16 = 65 = 13 × 5
01 · Observe
In each case, how do the two factors of the result relate to the original two numbers? Look at 8×2 and 5,3. Look at 12×2 and 7,5. What do you notice?
02 · Conjecture
Complete: "a² − b² = (____) × (____)" where the blanks are expressions involving a and b.
03–04 · Verify & Justify
Expand (a + b)(a − b) algebraically. What do you get? This is your justification — explain why it proves your conjecture for all values of a and b, including decimals and negatives.
Bands 7–8
Conjecture: a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)

Justification by expansion: (a + b)(a − b) = a² − ab + ab − b² = a² − b² ✓

This algebraic identity holds for all real values of a and b — not just integers. The middle terms (−ab and +ab) always cancel, leaving exactly a² − b². There is no restriction on a or b, so this cannot fail for any numerical case.
Grade 10 Unit 1 · Finance Matters Sequences · Further
Geometric series and convergence
The pattern
Consider the series: 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 + ...

Partial sums: S₁=1, S₂=1.5, S₃=1.75, S₄=1.875, S₅=1.9375...

What does Sₙ approach as n → ∞?
01 · Observe
What value do the partial sums appear to approach? How much does each new term add? What is the common ratio r?
02 · Conjecture
Conjecture: S∞ = ____. State the formula for infinite geometric series and the condition required for it to converge.
03–04 · Verify & Justify
Let S = a + ar + ar² + ar³ + ... Write rS. Subtract rS from S. What do you get? Use this to derive S∞ = a/(1−r) and explain why the condition |r| < 1 is essential — what happens to the formula if r = 1 or r = −2?
Bands 7–8 · Algebraic derivation with conditions
Let S = a + ar + ar² + ar³ + ...
Then rS = ar + ar² + ar³ + ar⁴ + ...
Subtracting: S − rS = a (all other terms cancel)
S(1 − r) = a, so S = a/(1−r)

For this series: a = 1, r = 1/2 → S∞ = 1/(1 − 0.5) = 2 ✓

Why |r| < 1 is essential: The subtraction step assumes the tail of the series vanishes as n → ∞. This only happens when |r| < 1 — each term becomes smaller. If r = 1, the series is 1+1+1+... which diverges. If r = −2, terms grow in magnitude (1, −2, 4, −8...) and the formula gives 1/(1−(−2)) = 1/3, which is meaningless — the series doesn't converge to anything.

Pick a pattern, write your own justification, then reveal the model answer at each band level. Compare honestly — which band does your response actually sit at?

The sum of two consecutive integers is always odd
Grades 7–8 Number
The pattern 3 + 4 = 7    10 + 11 = 21    99 + 100 = 199    0 + 1 = 1
All results are odd. Write a justification that proves this must always be true — not just for these examples.
Write your own attempt before revealing — that's the whole point.
Bands 1–2 · Observation only

"I tried 3+4=7 (odd), 10+11=21 (odd), 50+51=101 (odd). It always seems to work."

Why this scores 1–2: No conjecture is stated for all cases. No reason is given. More examples cannot raise this score — you need to say something about why it must always happen.

Bands 3–4 · Conjecture stated, verified

"I think the sum of any two consecutive integers is always odd. I tested: 7+8=15, 20+21=41, 100+101=201, 1000+1001=2001. All odd. My conjecture holds for all cases I checked."

Why this scores 3–4: Conjecture is clear and applies to all cases. Verified with new examples. But checking more examples is still not justification — you haven't explained why it must be true.

Bands 5–6 · Partial justification

"Consecutive integers always include one even and one odd number. When you add an even and an odd number, you always get an odd number. So the sum of two consecutive integers must be odd."

Why this scores 5–6: Uses mathematical structure — not just examples. The reasoning is sound. To reach 7–8, you need to explain why even + odd always equals odd, not just assert it.

Bands 7–8 · Full justification

"Let the smaller integer be n. The consecutive pair is n and n+1. Their sum is n + (n+1) = 2n + 1. Since 2n is always divisible by 2 (and therefore even), adding 1 always produces an odd number. This holds for every integer n — positive, negative, or zero — without exception."

Why this scores 7–8: Uses algebra to represent all cases simultaneously. The conclusion follows necessarily from the algebra. No counterexample is possible because the argument covers all integers.

The difference between consecutive square numbers is always odd
Grades 6–8 Number · Algebra
The pattern 2² − 1² = 3    3² − 2² = 5    4² − 3² = 7    5² − 4² = 9
The differences are 3, 5, 7, 9 — all odd. Justify why this must always be the case.
Bands 1–2

"The differences go 3, 5, 7, 9 which are all odd numbers. The next difference should be 11."

Why 1–2: Continues the pattern but makes no general claim. No attempt to say why or for how many cases.

Bands 3–4

"I think the difference between any two consecutive square numbers is always odd. I checked: 6²−5²=11, 10²−9²=19, 20²−19²=39. All odd. My conjecture is correct."

Why 3–4: Clear conjecture, verified with new cases. Still no structural reason.

Bands 5–6

"The differences between consecutive squares are 3, 5, 7, 9 — they form an arithmetic sequence of odd numbers going up by 2. The nth difference appears to be 2n+1, which is always odd because it equals an even number plus 1."

Why 5–6: Formula identified from the pattern. Partially justified (2n+1 is odd). To reach 7–8, derive the formula algebraically rather than reading it from the sequence.

Bands 7–8

"Let the two consecutive square numbers be n² and (n+1)². Their difference is:
(n+1)² − n² = n² + 2n + 1 − n² = 2n + 1
Since 2n is always even, 2n + 1 is always odd — for every integer n. The differences also increase by 2 each time because when n increases by 1, 2n+1 increases by 2."

Why 7–8: Algebraic derivation from first principles. The formula 2n+1 is not read from the pattern — it is derived by expanding the brackets. Covers all integers.

Interior angle of a regular polygon
Grade 7 Geometry
The pattern Triangle: 60°    Square: 90°    Pentagon: 108°    Hexagon: 120°
The formula appears to be (n−2) × 180° ÷ n. Justify where this formula comes from — not just that it works.
Bands 1–2

"A triangle has 60°, a square has 90°, a pentagon has 108°. The angles get bigger as you add more sides."

Bands 3–4

"The formula is (n−2)×180÷n. For a heptagon (7 sides): (7−2)×180÷7 = 900÷7 ≈ 128.6°. I verified this by drawing and measuring — it looks right."

Why 3–4: Formula stated and verified. But "it looks right" is not a reason. Where does the formula come from?

Bands 5–6

"You can split any polygon into triangles. A triangle makes 1 triangle, a square makes 2, a pentagon makes 3. So an n-sided polygon makes n−2 triangles. Total angle sum = (n−2)×180°. Since all angles in a regular polygon are equal, each angle = (n−2)×180°÷n."

Why 5–6: Correct reasoning about the triangle decomposition. To reach 7–8, explain why a polygon always produces exactly n−2 triangles — not just observe the pattern 1, 2, 3...

Bands 7–8

"From one vertex of an n-sided polygon, you can draw diagonals to all non-adjacent vertices. You cannot draw to the 2 adjacent vertices (those are sides) or to yourself — that leaves n−3 diagonals, creating n−2 triangles. Each triangle has an interior angle sum of 180°, so the total angle sum of any n-sided polygon is (n−2)×180°. Since it is a regular polygon, all n angles are equal: each = (n−2)×180°÷n. This works for any n ≥ 3."

Why 7–8: The n−2 is derived, not observed. The exclusion of adjacent vertices is explained. The condition n≥3 is stated. Full structural justification.

The difference of two squares: a² − b² = (a+b)(a−b)
Grade 9 Algebra
The pattern 5²−3² = 16 = 8×2    7²−4² = 33 = 11×3    10²−6² = 64 = 16×4
In each case the two factors are (a+b) and (a−b). Justify this algebraic identity for all values.
Bands 1–2

"5²−3²=16 and (5+3)(5−3)=8×2=16. It works."

Why 1–2: One example confirmed. No conjecture, no claim about all cases.

Bands 3–4

"I think a²−b² always equals (a+b)(a−b). I checked: 9²−4²=65=(13)(5)✓, 12²−7²=95=(19)(5)✓, 20²−3²=391=(23)(17)✓. The pattern holds every time I test it."

Why 3–4: Clear conjecture, multiple verifications including larger numbers. No structural reason given.

Bands 5–6

"(a+b)(a−b) = a×a − a×b + b×a − b×b = a² − ab + ab − b² = a² − b². The two middle terms cancel."

Why 5–6: Correct algebraic expansion. The answer follows logically. To reach 7–8, explain what the cancellation tells you and confirm it holds for all real values of a and b including negative and equal values.

Bands 7–8

"Expand (a+b)(a−b) using the distributive law:
(a+b)(a−b) = a(a−b) + b(a−b) = a²−ab+ba−b² = a²−b²
The terms −ab and +ba are equal and opposite, so they always cancel — regardless of the values of a and b. This means a²−b² = (a+b)(a−b) for all real numbers a and b, including negative values (e.g. a=−3, b=5: 9−25=−16=(2)(−8)✓) and when a=b (both sides equal zero)."

Why 7–8: Derivation from the distributive law — not just FOIL without explanation. The cancellation is explained, not just observed. Edge cases (negative values, a=b) are explicitly tested.

Year-Level Benchmarks

What a Level 7–8 Criterion B response looks like changes across the MYP years. The expectation escalates — here's exactly how.

Grade What "Justify" means at this level Example of acceptable justification
Grade 6 A clear reason using words and/or a diagram. Does not need to be algebraic. Shows the student understands why — not just that — the pattern holds. "Triangles tessellate because each interior angle is 60°. Six triangles meeting at a point give 6×60°=360°, which fills the space exactly without gaps."
Grade 7 Reasoning that addresses all cases, not just drawn examples. May use simple algebraic notation. Geometric justifications should reference angle properties by name. "Vertically opposite angles must be equal because both pairs of angles on a straight line sum to 180°. If a+b=180° and a+c=180°, then b=c."
Grade 8 Algebraic argument expected where the pattern is numerical. "Let n represent..." language. Justification should work for negative numbers and zero where relevant. "Let the even number be 2m and the odd number be 2k+1. Their sum is 2m+2k+1 = 2(m+k)+1, which is always odd for any integers m and k."
Grade 9 Full algebraic derivation. Justification connects to the mathematical structure — not just the result. Student should anticipate and address edge cases or conditions. "(a+b)(a−b) = a²−ab+ab−b² = a²−b². The middle terms always cancel, so this identity holds for all real a and b, including a=b (giving 0=0) and negative values."
Grade 10 Derivation from first principles. Conditions stated and explained. Justification should demonstrate why the formula fails outside stated conditions — not just that it works within them. "S∞ = a/(1−r) is derived by subtracting rS from S. This requires the series to have a finite limit, which only occurs when |r|<1 — otherwise the rⁿ term does not vanish as n→∞, and the derivation is invalid."

The thread across all years

6–7
Justification uses words, diagrams, and geometric reasoning. The key is showing the student understands the structure — not just the result.
8–9
Justification uses algebraic variables. "Let n represent any integer" is the move that separates Grades 8–9 justification from Grades 6–7.
10
Justification uses derivation and conditions. A Grade 10 justification doesn't just prove the formula — it explains when the formula fails and why. This is the beginning of mathematical maturity.
