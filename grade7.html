Criteria Guide

Grade 7 is the year you build your mathematical habits. The criteria aren't just about marks — they're about how you think, communicate, and make sense of mathematics.

A

Knowing & Understanding

Select and apply with confidence

Crit A in Grade 7 is about accurately applying the right technique to the right problem. With four units covering percentages, sets, algebra, and geometry, you're building a toolkit — Criterion A tests whether you can reach for the right tool.

The key difference between Level 5–6 and Level 7–8 at this stage is consistency and accuracy across unfamiliar contexts. A problem that looks like a ratio question but is actually about compound interest is unfamiliar. A geometry question in an architectural context is unfamiliar. Can you adapt what you know?

Level 5–6
Solves familiar problems correctly most of the time. Some errors in more complex multi-step questions. Generally selects an appropriate method.
Level 7–8
Accurately solves both familiar and unfamiliar problems. Selects the most efficient method. Answers are complete, correctly stated, and include appropriate units.
B

Investigating Patterns

Predict, generalise, and justify

Crit B is the main criterion for Unit 2 (Logic) and Unit 4 (Geometry). You're expected to look at examples, identify what's happening, and describe the pattern in a way that works for all cases — not just the ones you've seen.

In Grade 7, "justification" is beginning to emerge as a requirement. At Level 7–8, you're expected to explain why a pattern works — not just state what it is. For example, in tessellations: it's not enough to say "triangles tessellate." You need to explain why — interior angles sum to 180°, and three triangles meeting at a point sum to 360°.

Level 5–6
Identifies the pattern correctly. States a general rule. Verifies with additional examples. Some attempt at justification.
Level 7–8
Pattern identified and described precisely. General rule stated correctly for all cases. Justified — not just verified. Explanation addresses why the pattern must hold, not just that it does.
C

Communicating

Clear, precise, and mathematical

Criterion C in Grade 7 focuses on using the correct mathematical language and notation for the first time across several new symbol systems — set notation, algebraic notation, and coordinate geometry.

A common mistake: writing answers in words when mathematical notation is expected. Write P(A∪B) not "the probability of A or B." Write y = 2x + 3 not "the line goes up by 2 each time and starts at 3." Precision in notation is a habit you build now and use for the rest of your mathematical education.

Also: every diagram needs labels. A Venn diagram without a universal set boundary and labelled sets, or a coordinate graph without axis labels, loses Crit C marks.

Level 5–6
Mostly uses correct notation. Working is present and generally clear. Some diagrams labelled. Conclusions present.
Level 7–8
Precise notation throughout. All diagrams fully labelled. Working is complete with no unexplained jumps. Conclusions are mathematically stated and reference the context.
D

Applying in Real Life

Model, solve, interpret, reflect

Crit D in Grade 7 introduces the four-stage process you'll use for the next six years: identify a real-life problem → build a mathematical model → apply it → reflect on the results. Units 1 and 3 are the primary Crit D units.

The most important thing at this level: interpret your answer in context. If you calculate that an exchange rate gives you 2.3 million Vietnamese dong for $100 AUD, don't just write "2,300,000." Write: "This means $100 AUD is worth approximately 2.3 million VND, which is enough to buy [X] in Vietnam."

Level 7–8 also requires you to identify at least one limitation of your model — what assumption did you make, and what might be different in reality?

Level 5–6
Applies mathematics to a real-life context. Interprets the answer in context. Solution is mostly complete.
Level 7–8
Identifies the mathematical structure of the problem. Applies an appropriate and complete model. Interprets results precisely in context. Identifies at least one limitation or assumption of the model.

Number & Ratio

Percent Increase & Decrease

Percent change = (change ÷ original) × 100. Increase: multiply by (1 + r/100). Decrease: multiply by (1 − r/100).

Worked example
A shirt costs $40, reduced by 15%:
$40 × 0.85 = $34
Number & Ratio

Simple vs Compound Interest

Simple: I = Prt (interest is always on the original principal). Compound: A = P(1 + r)ⁿ (interest earns interest each period).

Worked example
$1000 at 5% for 3 years:
Simple: I = 1000 × 0.05 × 3 = $150
Compound: A = 1000 × 1.05³ = $1157.63
Number & Ratio

Ratios & the Unitary Method

Simplify ratios by dividing by the HCF. Unitary method: find the value of 1 unit, then scale up. To divide in a ratio, find total parts first.

Worked example
Divide $120 in ratio 3:5:
Total parts = 8 · Each part = $15
→ $45 and $75
Number & Ratio

Exchange Rates & Metric Conversion

Multiply or divide by the conversion factor. For exchange rates, identify whether you are buying or selling and which direction the rate applies.

Worked example
1 USD = 25,000 VND. Convert $80 USD:
80 × 25,000 = 2,000,000 VND
Sets & Probability

Set Notation & Venn Diagrams

∪ = union (in A or B or both). ∩ = intersection (in both A and B). A' = complement (not in A). ∅ = empty set. |A| = cardinality (number of elements).

Reading a Venn diagram
n(A∪B) = n(A) + n(B) − n(A∩B)
If n(A)=10, n(B)=8, n(A∩B)=3:
n(A∪B) = 10+8−3 = 15
Sets & Probability

Probability & Complementary Events

P(event) = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes. Complementary: P(A') = 1 − P(A). All probabilities sum to 1.

Worked example
P(rolling a 6) = 1/6
P(not rolling a 6) = 1 − 1/6 = 5/6
Sets & Probability

Shading Venn Regions

To shade A'∪B: shade everything NOT in A, then also shade everything in B. Work step by step — first apply the complement, then the union or intersection.

Strategy
A'∪B: shade outside A ∪ shade inside B
(A∩B)': shade everything outside A∩B
= everything except the overlap
Algebra

Collecting Like Terms & Expanding

Like terms have the same variable and power. Collect them by adding or subtracting coefficients. Expand brackets by multiplying each term inside.

Worked example
3x² + 2x + 5x² + 3xy − 9 = 8x² + 2x + 3xy − 9
3(2x − 4) = 6x − 12
Algebra

Solving Equations

Keep the equation balanced — whatever you do to one side, do to the other. Expand brackets first, then collect like terms, then isolate the unknown.

Worked example
3(2x − 1) = 2x + 9
6x − 3 = 2x + 9
4x = 12 → x = 3
Algebra

Rearranging Formulas

To change the subject, use inverse operations in reverse order — treat the formula like an equation and isolate the target variable.

Worked example
Make r the subject of A = πr²:
r² = A/π → r = √(A/π)
Algebra

Linear Equations & y = mx + c

m = gradient (slope) — how much y changes per unit increase in x. c = y-intercept — where the line crosses the y-axis. A steeper line has a larger |m|.

Worked example
Line through (0, 3) and (2, 7):
m = (7−3)/(2−0) = 2 · c = 3
→ y = 2x + 3
Geometry

Angle Properties

Angles at a point = 360°. Angles on a straight line = 180°. Complementary = sum to 90°. Supplementary = sum to 180°. Vertically opposite angles are equal.

Quick reference
a + b = 180° (straight line)
a + b + c + d = 360° (at a point)
Vertically opposite: equal, always
Geometry

Parallel Line Angle Properties

Corresponding angles are equal (F shape). Alternate angles are equal (Z shape). Co-interior angles are supplementary — they add to 180° (C shape).

Memory trick
F = corresponding (equal)
Z = alternate (equal)
C = co-interior (add to 180°)
Geometry

Transformations

Translation (slide), reflection (flip), rotation (turn), enlargement (scale). Translations, reflections, and rotations preserve size and shape — the image is congruent to the object. Enlargements change size.

Congruence check
Translation → congruent ✓
Reflection → congruent ✓
Rotation → congruent ✓
Enlargement → similar, not congruent
Geometry

Volume & Surface Area

Volume of cuboid = l × w × h. Volume of prism = cross-sectional area × length. Surface area = sum of all face areas. Always state units² for area and units³ for volume.

Worked example
Cuboid 4cm × 3cm × 5cm:
Volume = 4×3×5 = 60 cm³
Surface area = 2(12+20+15) = 94 cm²
